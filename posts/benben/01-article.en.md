## Foreword

Hey ~ To commemorate my first post, please allow me to introduce myself a little XD

Hi everyone, I'm Benben. I love rational trivia and the emotional side of people and things.

According to the bell-shaped normal distribution in statistics, about 5% of people will misread "emotional" as "sexy."

Just kidding, I lied! What? Lying in the very first introduction — does this author want to be banned?

Wait... don't leave just yet!

I do have some basis for it, though. You see, there's a term in statistics called standard deviation (I won't go into detail here XD). If we treat misreading one character as one standard deviation, then misreading two characters is two standard deviations, and two standard deviations gives you a 95% confidence interval (not going into detail here either XD), so roughly 5% of people will misread it.

After all that nonsense, what on earth am I on about?

You ask where the normal distribution ever comes in handy? Honestly, it rarely does in everyday life.

But that's exactly the kind of cold trivia I'm after (?). Yep, I just like being different from everyone else.

How much do I love cold trivia? Take a look at my other cold trivia (skills):

- Learned bass (the least popular instrument in a band, RIP)
- Learned vim for writing code (probably the hardest editor to get into)
- Learned the Boshiamy input method (also super rarely used)

That's about it. Not top-tier, but not bad either. Feel free to chat, but no flame wars please XD

And the emotional side?

- I make handwritten cards and handwritten bookmarks (the kind written with a fountain pen)
- I occasionally keep a diary and such (less often these days)
- I cry listening to songs or watching movies (oh my! So embarrassing)

To all the men out there — do you know how to make a girl's heart flutter now? (Wait, you mean with a man's tears?)

I actually debated whether to write this section. It feels a bit affected and a bit funny (?), but heaven knows, earth knows, you know, and I know: "self-introduction" really is an important skill. It's just like "you can never have too much learning when you need it." Compared to something like: "I'm XXX, graduated from OO University, 18 years old this year, I know HTML/CSS and a little JavaScript," a good self-introduction can make an interviewer open their eyes just a little wider — because these days everyone has the same skillset. HTML/CSS/JavaScript (taking frontend as an example): I know them, you know them, even the guy next door knows them.

Of course, my self-introduction for future job hunting won't be written like this. I went with a more lighthearted approach here to introduce myself. If you're struggling with how to write your self-introduction or autobiography, I hope this gives you some inspiration — at the very least, you can write a pretentious version or a bragging version like I did, and refine it later XD

> That's my brief introduction. See you next time! Now, onto the main topic.

## What is an article?

First, let's talk about what an article even is.

Everyone wrote essays as a kid — there'd be a topic and around 500 to 600 words of text. I scored a 6 on my old entrance exam essay (out of a max of 12). Yep, I'm terrible at writing, which is exactly why I'm here to teach everyone how to write (?). Anyway, there's a topic, a structure, a style — the key thing is there's a lot of text. Unless you suffer, like me, from "the disease where you die if you read more than ten lines," you probably don't think 600 words is too many. So does lots of text count as an article? I think it depends on the person. Plenty of official documents go on forever — are those articles? That feels like another debate waiting to happen — wait, don't start that debate!

Broadly speaking, I'd say official docs, technical articles, clickbait farm articles, notes, and shitposts can all count as articles. What I want you to think about is: "What distinguishes them from one another?"

So what is it? Honestly, I don't know — it really varies from person to person. Knowledge density, depth of vocabulary, article structure — all differ, and everyone feels differently about them. To give you a clearer picture, I've very subjectively put together a table for reference:

|                   | Official docs | Technical articles | Clickbait farm | Notes | Shitposts |
| ----------------- | ------------- | ------------------ | -------------- | ----- | --------- |
| Knowledge density | 5             | 4                  | 2              | 3     | 1         |
| Vocabulary depth  | 5             | 4                  | 2              | 3     | 1         |
| Fun factor        | 1             | 2                  | 4              | 3     | 5         |
| Amount of text    | 5             | 4                  | 3              | 2     | ?         |
| Article structure | 2             | 5                  | 4              | 3     | 1         |

Right, articles can be really complex — structure, content, word choice, audience, and so on. Naturally, there are tons of writing tutorials online and plenty of articles on the benefits of writing, so I won't dwell on those. Maybe you've thought about writing or starting a blog, and you've even read lots of tutorials by the masters. You get the principles — but... you just can't seem to produce anything! Yeah, that's how a lot of people feel. Including me, of course.

> Further reading: [How do I write an article? | by Huli](https://hulitw.medium.com/how-do-i-write-965328ae91fe)

For someone who isn't in the habit of writing, completing a full article is just so, so hard...

But! Everyone starts somewhere. The masters all began with garbage and shitposts — they just started earlier. If you start now, will you become amazing? I don't know. But if you want to be amazing, you absolutely have to start!

So I think my goal here is: to help people who aren't used to writing build the habit of writing, and maybe even produce a simple article.

I won't go too deep — that'd be too complicated. I might not explain it clearly, and you might not understand. So let's approach it from somewhere simpler.

"When you see a wall of text, what do you think matters most?"

Setting content aside, if there are no punctuation marks, you can skip it entirely. Honestly, punctuation is itself a kind of structure, and one that should be used carefully.

> Further reading: [Chinese Copywriting Guidelines](https://github.com/sparanoid/chinese-copywriting-guidelines)

OK, once you have the structure of punctuation, words become sentences, and if we step up another level, we get paragraphs within an article. Articles absolutely need to be broken into paragraphs. Remember **introduction, development, twist, conclusion** from essay class? Exactly — let's just hold onto that principle and skip the rest!

I've said a lot above, but my one and only point is this: yes, "structure."

## Notes

"I'm a junior engineer — do I need to take notes?"

"Of course you do!"

Unless you're some kind of genius with a photographic memory, in which case feel free to hit the back button XD

If you don't yet have a writing habit, the best way in is notes. You can jot down simple things, and it helps your memory — a true win-win.

But what if I'm not great at note-taking either? Don't worry. Try first introducing the idea of structure into your notes. A simple list (or calling it a `li element` might be clearer) is already very useful. You can check out the book _Bullet Journal_, or if you're familiar with HTML, you can apply the DOM model's structure to your notes. It's really nothing fancy — everyone gets it — but the reality is: "a lot of people's notes are still a chaotic mess." Maybe try adding some "structure" and see — it might improve things a lot.

> Recommended book: [The Bullet Journal Method](https://www.books.com.tw/products/0010803003)

The most basic note structure looks like this:

```markdown
### My frontend skills

- HTML
- CSS
- JavaScript
```

Yes, you can literally open Notepad and write it like that, though it won't look great in Notepad. But if you know the `Markdown` syntax, you'll get what I'm doing. I highly recommend practicing notes and `Markdown` syntax on `HackMD`. If you've never used it, why not start now? It's billed as a 10-minute quick start.

> HackMD: [HackMD quick start tutorial](https://hackmd.io/c/tutorials-tw/%2Fs%2Fquick-start-tw)

OK, that's it for simple notes. Back to the article part.

I've recently been taking part in a programming mentorship program, a course for people transitioning into engineering. Since it's online, one rule is that you have to submit 5 progress reports a week. "Report" sounds intimidating, but you can really write about anything — the point is just to let the instructor know you're still breathing and alive. That's not the focus here, though. A friend of mine felt progress reports were hard to write, so one day they wrote:

"It's so hot today ~~~"

Yeah, just five characters. Hold your laughter, everyone. Writing something **every single day** is genuinely hard — you'd know if you'd tried.

But do you know what's even more of a nothing-post?

"Like!"

That's right — Facebook's "Like," a button where the more you use it, the more you lose your ability to think. You might wonder if it's really that serious. It is!

First off, a tiny Like icon only reveals the user's name and "Like" — it offers no real help to the poster, just empty like counts that make it seem like you're getting tons of attention. But the moment you're alone, that moment feels oddly flat. I'm sure many people have felt this.

Then there are the people who do all sorts of unhinged things for the sake of like counts — too many to list. If we could rewind to 2008, who'd have guessed that a dozen-plus years later Facebook would influence us this much? One extra "Like" and suddenly you feel like you've beaten someone a little. If you also want to break free of "like addiction," this book might help.

> Recommended book: [Digital Minimalism](https://www.books.com.tw/products/0010843512)

Have you ever seen the worst kind of reviewer?

"I think it's tasty," "I think it's good," "I think it's great"... Sound familiar? It feels like they're just phoning it in, right? If so, I think that's great — because you've already started noticing this is a problem. There's no shortcut to fixing it; you just have to think more and express yourself more.

When did we lose our ability to express ourselves? That might be overstating it, but it's true that most people never specifically cultivated independent thinking while growing up. And people who write well usually think well, because you need independent thinking to express your own ideas — and to express them in a way others can understand.

Let's go back to the earlier "It's so hot today ~~~"

Let's try turning it into a short piece. How would we do that?

1. Add knowledge points
2. Add a title
3. Add a personal story

```markdown
### A few words on the weather

The day I met you was a muggy July day, and now July has come around again.
It's hot today too — not sure if it's a record high, but today (2021/07/13) Taiwan's electricity usage hit a new high.
See: https://money.udn.com/money/story/5648/5598949
```

Doesn't that look like something now? The content's still a bit thin, though XD

But content is the most important part of an article. What is the article trying to express?

Remember those notes from earlier? Exactly — content is built up from what you accumulate day to day. You can record it in notes. Really anything is fair game for notes: reflections and feelings, programming notes, food and travel journals... You can note anything down, and it all becomes your own capital later. When inspiration strikes, an article just kind of slips out.

Toward the end, the hardest thing to come up with is often the title. Here are some common article title patterns — if you can't think of one, just pick one to use XD

Common article titles: "A few words on...", "An introduction to...", "A deep-but-accessible guide to...", "Starting from...", "What you don't know about...", "Should I learn...?", "...for beginners", and so on.

But please don't do these things in articles:

1. Copy-pasting more than 20% of someone else's article. With that approach, copying 20% from each of 5 articles and you'd be done — wait, huh (?)
2. Not citing your references. This is a pretty serious issue, and a lot of people overlook it — but for the original author it's just basic respect.

And there you have it — an article has accidentally come into being. If you want to learn more writing techniques, I highly recommend checking out the references below.

## Finally

I'm not sure whether this counts as a technical article, but writing is definitely a skill. Let's recap what we covered today:

We have the basic structure of an article (introduction, development, twist, conclusion), templates for titles, and little reminders about writing. What's left is your thinking and expression ability — that part takes time to cultivate, and Taiwan's education system rarely focuses on it. So the next time you hit Like, don't just hit Like — pause for a moment and think more: what's your feeling in that moment? Why do you feel this post deserves a Like? Try putting it into your own words.

Reading to here, you probably have a different take on "writing" — like, maybe writing isn't actually that hard. Or maybe you don't feel that way — well, I don't know. Everyone's background is different, but that's genuinely how I slowly, bit by bit, built my writing habit. Thanks for reading this far!

Maybe you can write something right now, or jot down a simple shitpost reflection (?)
