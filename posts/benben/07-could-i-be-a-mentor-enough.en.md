## Foreword

! **This post leans toward the mindset and self-improvement side, and also records a story.**

Time flies — before I knew it, half of 2022 is already over! My annual goals are more or less on track. For the second half I'll be leveling up my English. I've been talking about it for ages, so at the end of June I signed up for the [VoiceTube Challenge](https://hero.voicetube.com/challenge-youth) with friends (10%-off code: v_rnc0xg). Will my broken English actually pull off the challenge? See you in three months.

I'm a student of [Lidemy's fifth cohort](https://bootcamp.lidemy.com/) (and possibly the last one?), and it's been over half a year since I switched into frontend. I won't go into the bloody saga of my career change here — happy to have met all the greats at Lidemy.

Even though I'm already a frontend engineer, I still keep up a continuous-learning mindset. As for how long my passion for the frontend field will burn, I don't know — but it's been burning for 1 to 2 years now, and barring any surprises, it'll keep going. To become a senior frontend engineer, the "leadership" skill is basically mandatory. So what do you do when everyone at your company is a master, and there's no one to lead?

You have to go find it yourself (?).

And it turns out, I was lucky enough to be found by someone — hence this post. Is it fate? I don't know.

The person who found me is K. We met on the [Zero To Mastery (ZTM)](https://zerotomastery.io/community/developer-community-discord) community. ZTM's courses are also great — it's a hugely popular series on Udemy, but I won't dwell on that here. Basically, the community is almost all foreigners, so running into another Taiwanese person is genuinely lucky!

K said he'd also found 2–3 juniors who were still learning, and was planning to start a study group for mutual learning. People with experience were very welcome, so I smoothly "snuck in" and started my "micro-mentor journey."

Why did I want to start a "micro-mentor program"? Let's keep reading~

## A Single Spark Starts a Prairie Fire

So who I am isn't important. What's important is that a long time ago, in a galaxy far, far away ...

(A long time ago in a galaxy far, far away....)

Back then, the frontend world was in the middle of all kinds of wars — the browser wars, the editor wars, the frontend framework wars — all sorts of wars (~~and of course Star Wars~~). Wars until your dad can't keep up, wars until your kids are born — in short, wars until you can't even.

Amid the flames of war and the suffering of the people, one person launched the "Frontend Guide Program."

> Further reading: [A Real Mentorship Program: The Frontend Guide Program | by Huli | Medium](https://hulitw.medium.com/mentorship-program-350db93d5c9c)

The "Frontend Guide Program" is a free activity where a "mentor" leads other engineers to grow together. It's not limited to frontend, and the projects aren't restricted either — fairly freeform. Most importantly: it's free!

This tiny spark is a small lamp of light in this war-torn frontend world. The early stages of the Guide Program may not have had ideal results, but every small step was the foundation that would eventually start a prairie fire — and the predecessor of what would become the Programming Mentorship Program.

From very early on, taking just about 5 people, to the fifth cohort where nearly 100 signed up — because of the TA system they could admit more students. About 25 people successfully completed it (here meaning: found an engineering job within 2 months of finishing the course — though there are more, since many found jobs later, just a bit delayed). And those who didn't succeed didn't have to pay tuition either, since it's a guaranteed program — guaranteed career change — but how much you learn is, of course, up to your own cultivation.

## Kindred Spirits

Afterwards I successfully joined Lidemy's Frontend Mentorship Program, fifth cohort. I was extremely happy and felt like the luckiest person in the world.

Because someone as good as the teacher is rare in this world. Only after entering society do you understand the cruelty of reality — why should a senior teach you anything? "Teach the apprentice, starve the master."

Besides, people who genuinely want to learn are few and far between. There's also no shortage of "gimme" types who are just window-shopping. But those who join the Programming Mentorship Program are pre-screened. "I see you've got the right stuff — a once-in-a-century martial-arts prodigy. Want to become a ~~liver-busting~~ engineer?"

Uh... sure!

In short, being able to fight alongside a group of people with goals and ideals feels fantastic!

What's a bit different: this program **has no fixed Final Project.** Generally, most bootcamps have a planned Final Project — most commonly an e-commerce shopping cart, or a Twitter or Netflix clone. So finding like-minded people to do the Final Project together is itself a rare and precious thing. Plus the whole project is from scratch — from user story planning, figma mockups, frontend and backend implementation, all the way to deployment — it's all just you and your teammates. You can ask TAs or the teacher when you have questions, but basically you have to complete it independently with your team. It's hard work, but finishing it gives you a real sense of accomplishment!

Honestly, the "kindred spirits" here isn't just the teacher — it's also the teammates and classmates. Tiny me being able to find such an environment, classmates, and TAs is genuinely precious.

Those days of writing code until the middle of the night — I'll probably never forget them.

## Passing the Torch

In the original post for the teacher's founding program, it says:

> If, after this year, you're grateful to me for mentoring you for free, don't thank me. Take that gratitude and start the same kind of program to help others — that's the best return.

The condition is:

> After a year, you also have to start the same program, taking no fewer than 2 people.

Although I'm not very senior, I wanted to give back to the teacher. And who knew — I thought about it, and then it happened. It's just that magical.

Next comes my part in passing the torch. Let me briefly introduce this "micro-mentor" program. We mainly use Discord as our communication tool, but when sharing we use Google Meet to avoid lag.

Current members (using initials here):

- K — the organizer, a powerhouse self-learner. He's currently at a foreign semiconductor company, writing Python to process data. He's basically already seen React, has gone through most of it, and is grinding through some LeetCode. I think he could switch careers anytime XD.

- S — currently still a student, wants to head toward blockchain in the future. Learning Solidity, and has also used Next.js for the frontend. Her grasp of React is still not high (?her words), but she's not really aiming for frontend anyway — she might head toward grad school.

- A — currently taking classes at III, learning PHP backend. Plans to finish the course this year and job-hunt at year-end. Also looking at some PM courses — might move toward PM in the future.

- Me — useless frontend newbie. Seeing everyone so serious, I'm genuinely embarrassed QQ.

OK fine, maybe I'm not that useless. Still, I should learn to bide my time, dim my own light, and leave the time and space for these rising stars to shine! Just kidding, I just want to be a slacker.

See you in half a year, fellow software engineers.

Let this tiny spark (torch) keep burning.
