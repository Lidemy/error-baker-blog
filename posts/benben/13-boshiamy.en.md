## Awakening

When it comes to Boshiamy, most people picture something only taught in junior/high school computer class (revealing my age). People who use it must be old, right? But I've only learned it for about 2 years, and I can already chat and write articles fluently. Sure, sometimes I still hit characters I can't type fast, but for me, the pros outweigh the cons.

It started with Mr. Papaya's intro video — seeing him type Boshiamy "fast, fierce, and precise," I was instantly hooked. I rewatched the video several times, then steeled myself to learn it. Counting from now, I started learning from scratch about 2 years ago.

Before that, I was a complete newbie. The only time I'd touched Boshiamy was back in computer class, accidentally switching to it and typing a few gibberish characters, and that was it. Maybe because I didn't have a computer as a kid, even my regular Zhuyin Chinese typing was slow. I figured since my Chinese typing wasn't fast anyway and the switching cost was low, I might as well switch and practice along the way.

What attracted me to Boshiamy, besides its biggest advantage of not needing to pick characters, is that you can do Chinese input with just "**A–Z, 26 letters total**." You might think that's nothing, but besides having fewer keys than Zhuyin (ㄅ, ..., 1st tone, ..., etc. — **41 total**), fewer inputs mean faster typing.

> Try typing: 「1 生、 2 人、 3 餐、 4 季」

Either you switch "Chinese/English" mode multiple times, or you use the standalone numeric keypad — both are more time-consuming. The farthest distance in the world is from the middle of the keyboard to the arrow keys (the standalone numeric keys are even farther). But **Boshiamy can type numbers directly**, because it doesn't occupy any number keys. Numbers are also a fun part of Boshiamy — I'll mention that later. What about other symbols, like `!@#+-()[]{}`? If you happen to need these symbols, in Boshiamy you don't need to switch at all — isn't that great?

Also as mentioned above, Boshiamy only needs 26 letters, but to type fast, your English typing also needs to be reasonably good. If you already type English fast, great. If not, that's also great — because mastering Boshiamy can **boost your English typing to some degree**. You can level them up together.

So based on all the reasons above, I decided to dive into Boshiamy (it's not just because it looks cool, really). Next, I'll talk about my learning process — maybe the most boring part but also the most important. I hope something above resonated.

## Hard Practice

Once I made up my mind, every day I went to the official site's online practice for 10–30 minutes. At first I really had no idea what I was doing — a bunch of radicals to memorize, plus English typing practice. I figured A–Z was only 26 letters, so it shouldn't be too hard. But some radicals combine form, sound, and meaning, which makes them feel hard. It might also be because I was juggling a lot back then — about 2 years ago I was in a full-time career-change program, learning Vim, practicing English typing, and practicing Boshiamy, so I learned slower. If you have more time and less other learning pressure, you can definitely be much faster than I was!

Although we say "memorize radicals," Boshiamy's radicals all have a basis. So **with a little imagination, many radicals basically don't need to be memorized**. Once you're familiar with the basic radicals, you can already start typing a bit.

Overall, I think the official practice site is pretty good — slowly going one by one from easy-to-remember radicals to those needing some imagination. I spent about 10–30 minutes a day, and after about a month, I could type 50% of characters. Mentally, don't think of it as hard — treat it like a game: today you learned "skill A," day by day you learn up to "skill Z," and once you've learned them all, you can unleash the combo "Eighteen Subduing Dragon Palms" (kidding).

## Migration

When you can type about 90% of characters and your speed is around 40, it's time to dive straight into using Boshiamy for Chinese input. Unless you're gaming and need to fire off trash-talk extremely fast, normal usage at around 60 is plenty. So really, **at around 40** you can dive straight in ~

The Boshiamy official site also thoughtfully provides a trial version. And the trial is very generous — **you can use it for free indefinitely**, just reinstall it every three months. When I first dove in, I started with the trial too. I used it for about a year before switching to the paid version (you can also hold off on buying — the article's end mentions free alternatives. But I do support the legit version).

Of course, after practicing all those radicals and complete characters, the next step is real combat — and real combat is the fastest way to boost speed. When I first switched over, I was also pretty clunky — definitely clunky. But it seems everyone goes through this, so there's nothing to blame. Just blame yourself for not being proficient enough and keep practicing!

I'd note down characters I often couldn't type or wasn't familiar with in a notes app (you're welcome to use them for practice too), practice them with the "shortest code," and practice them again the next day. Over time, these words become familiar one by one and slowly become reflexive.

## Typing Feels Great Now, Typing Feels Great Forever

When you can pretty much type every character, you think Boshiamy stops there? You're wrong! **The fun is just beginning!** If you're not yet fluent in one of the earlier stages and you're already reading this section, hold on a bit longer! At this stage, Boshiamy's true power shows — but the premise is you have to make it to this stage. You can go back and get more familiar with the radicals first.

In the Boshiamy world, there's a "one-radical table" — that is, A–Z. As the name suggests, **one English letter + space** types one Chinese character.

For example: `A => 對`, `D => 的`, etc. Characters like these can be typed lightning fast.

These are usually carefully picked common characters or common characters that are otherwise hard to type. Among them there's also a special "Chinese numerals" set — by Boshiamy's design, all done with one stroke. So it's highly recommended for readers who often need to type Chinese numbers, like in official documents, transcripts, etc. **Chinese numerals in Zhuyin have lots of homophones** and almost always ~~definitely~~ require picking characters.

```javascript
E => 一
R => 二
S => 三
F => 四
W => 五
L => 六
C => 七
B => 八
K => 九
J => 十
```

> Try reading them out and matching to the English — you'll discover a new world!

As the saying goes, "if there's one, there's two." Of course in the Boshiamy world there's also a "two-radical table" — AA, AB, ..., ZZ. As the name suggests, **two English letters + space** types one Chinese character. That means the two-radical table has 26 × 26 characters, usually short codes for common characters. Fun thing: one character can have multiple ways to type it — the regular one, the short code — but rarely needs picking.

So what's the use of all this Boshiamy stuff? The implication: "assuming equal proficiency, compared to Zhuyin, Boshiamy needs far fewer inputs to type a character. Which input method do you think is faster (and more accurate)?"

Beyond all of Boshiamy's advantages — no character picking, only English letters, etc. — from here on is **where Zhuyin can't catch up**. Typing one or two letters to complete one Chinese character is something Zhuyin can't do (well, it can, but it'd just be Zhuyin-lish, you know). So keep practicing your Boshiamy. If you can't type fast yet, that's fine — at least you're typing effortlessly and without picking characters. Get your speed to 50–60 first — that's plenty for now. If one day you want to push speed, you can come back and practice. You'll find that while you struggled with Zhuyin and always hit a bottleneck, Boshiamy breezes past — and even surpasses it!

## Bonus: rime-liur

`rime-liur` is a full-featured Boshiamy solution based on the `RIME` input method. `RIME` is also called "Squirrel" / "鼠鬚管" — if you become a "little shrimp" (small Boshiamy user), you'll probably hear about it.

> GitHub Repo: [hsuanyi-chou/rime-liur: A full-featured Boshiamy solution based on RIME](https://github.com/hsuanyi-chou/rime-liur)

`rime-liur` is developed by @hsuanyi-chou based on `RIME` and **is open source** (I've given it a Star to support it). For us regular users it's ~~basically free to use~~. So what's special about `rime-liur`? See the README on GitHub (runs away).

OK, here I'll highlight a few handy features:

1. You can directly use `shift` to toggle Chinese/English (usually only `ctrl + space` works).
2. Zhuyin mode, pinyin mode, character-code reverse lookup.
3. You can customize the input method's font, theme, candidate color, and so on.

A friend recently ran into trouble using the "add words/add characters" feature (this is actually an official feature, so I didn't mention it above). For non-developers, editing config files can be hard — a missing space can break it (truly). So I just recorded a quick tutorial for my friend — also a record for me. Interested readers can check it out.

<center>

[![嘸蝦米輸入法](https://img.youtube.com/vi/I9tlZFB2lvI/0.jpg)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I9tlZFB2lvI)

</center>

> Rime Boshiamy's add-word/add-character feature tutorial, by @Benben

For example: in the video, the default was `name + space` outputs `我的名字` (my name). I added `Benben` afterward, so from then on, `name + space` outputs `我的名字：Benben`. Adding names, emails, addresses and so on is just the basics. Later in the video I added `hw; + space` to output `Hello World` — taking h for hello and w for world, which should be easy to understand.

Sharp-eyed readers will notice: I added a `;` after `hw`. That's intentional, because in Boshiamy, combinations of 1–4 English letters have a high chance of collision (colloquially "taken"). So I added a symbol as a suffix. Of course, readers can also unleash their creativity. So I say: **the fun is just beginning!**

## Wrap-up

Boshiamy obviously isn't something you can pick up just by reading an article. Lots of practice is necessary, but what you put in will bear sweet fruit. What you save is your time. As the saying goes, "time is money." Suppose typing saves you 10 minutes a day — that's 5 hours a month. Putting that to better use isn't that great?

For example, if you're in customer service or sales, you have certain common phrases — you can add them all to Boshiamy's "add words/add characters" feature. Once set up, typing a few characters will spit them out like a machine gun. Other coworkers will be amazed. It's a great time-saver — highly recommended for anyone who often inputs large amounts of text.

Boshiamy is deep and has a steep learning curve, but the ceiling is also high and there's lots to play with. There are many Boshiamy variants, too — `rime-liur` in this article is just one of them. Lots of fun features and plugins await exploration.

Later I asked some seniors (Big Boshiamy users) — really grateful to them for sharing. I hope this "almost-lost input method" can be passed on. Such a great tool can't be known only to me!

Here's a compiled list of Boshiamy tricks:

1. `,,SP` fast mode (shortest code)
2. `,,C` Simplified Chinese mode
3. `,,CT` type-Traditional-output-Simplified mode
4. `,,J` Japanese mode
5. `,,T` switch back to Traditional mode
6. `,,X` secret-code decode mode
7. `、` homophone mode
8. `、；` Zhuyin input mode (one-time)
9. `,,box` add-character/add-word mode
10. Right-click > Properties, look up radicals or Zhuyin mode
