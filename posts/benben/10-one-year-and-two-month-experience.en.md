I'd meant to write this when I hit the one-year mark, but I was genuinely too busy then (yeah, I know I say that every time). So what was supposed to be a 1-year reflection got dragged out by another 2+ months (almost 3 now). **I'm an honest person** — 1 year 2 months is 1 year 2 months; I'm not going to call it "a 1-year reflection." After all, you can do a lot in 2 months. As for what you can do in 3 seconds... well, you know.

I'll try to write this reflection in plain language, so beginners, people mid-career-change, or non-specialists can all follow along — and I hope it helps. ## How Did I Dare

- Quit in under three months?

My first company was a digital design firm, K. They'd jumped on the "metaverse" craze, and I figured it had good growth potential. Only after joining did I realize I was wrong! The manager / PM didn't understand tech at all, and overtime was the norm. I can't really blame anyone, though — I did learn a lesson: when you see keywords like metaverse, cryptocurrency, NFT, etc., be careful. It might just be riding a buzzword for traffic. Research the company's background carefully.

At the same time, I observed as I worked. **The probation period isn't just the company evaluating you — you're also evaluating the company.** If it really feels wrong, switch quickly; don't waste time. And don't worry about how "less than a year" looks on your resume. Example: someone who stayed five years at one company — is that a good resume? Or are they just not skilled enough to leave, so they had to stay? You can't tell without working with them. Switching in under a year is really no big deal — think about how many "one years" you have to experiment with.

Later I moved to a pretty good small company, R. I wanted to leave K, so I opened up 104. It was right before Chinese New Year, and I got plenty of interview invites. Having the previous interview experience, I filtered out the obviously sketchy ones first. I talked a lot with R's CTO — 2 hours straight — and got an offer on the spot. The offer was good, so without much hesitation, I went.

- Take paid leave to interview again at six months?

Personally, I'm someone who **refreshes my resume every six months or so**. No need to over-explain — lots of experts have said this. Regularly checking opportunities also lets you see what you're still missing and where your value sits in the market. That's all helpful to you, so why not?

Worried about others' judgment? Forget it. How many friends actually stay in touch after you leave a job? Why let one glance hold back your future? But don't go overboard either — constantly wanting to switch right after joining, always browsing openings, always interviewing — the so-called "job-hopping itch." If you don't actually level up, in the long run you just become "really good at interviews." I'd suggest a frequency of around 3–6 months. Of course, if you're already at a top company or in a senior role, that may differ.

- Quick summary

Honestly, I was pretty lucky. R's CTO turned out to be a great manager — deep and broad technically, writing the app and acting as PM, not throwing out random requirements, not scheduling pointless meetings, completely leaving you alone after work — the group chat was so quiet you'd think no one was there. He treated the people under him pretty well, too. He was also really busy, though — small company! Normal.

I'm not saying I slacked off because I was leaving. Right up to the day before I resigned, I worked my full 8 hours, wrote docs, and cleaned up the old code. In the year I was there, I was never late or left early. I poured my heart into writing code for the company, and even when tasks were done, I was thinking about what to optimize. I can say I have a clear conscience about this job. I think that's enough. If I'd stayed longer, it might have been even harder to pull away.

Leaving R was going to happen sooner or later. My workload was almost 2–3x the other frontend dev's. Let me say it again: **I'm an honest person.** Of course the CTO was genuinely too busy and might not have noticed. The afternoon I resigned, I had a long chat with him about a lot of thoughts. He asked if I wanted to be a manager later. I immediately said yes! (Otherwise why do you think I keep leveling up? I want to be a manager or senior — hopefully!). He just said simply: "If so, doing a bit more is a good thing. His workload translates to about 5 people; I at best counted for 2 (couldn't manage 10 QQ). I don't know if I met his expectations in his mind, and I don't know if my choice was the right one — but who knows?

That day after work, riding my scooter home from Neihu over the MacArthur Bridge, the night sky was clear. Taipei 101 next to me was still glowing faint blue, slowly, blinking, blinking.

## What Right Did I Have

- Portfolio

Many frontend devs hate maintaining a portfolio. Me too!

Every time you switch platforms, you redo your resume and portfolio from scratch — name, birthday, address, and the most annoying: the bio. Just thinking about it is tiring, so I really hate reorganizing these things. In software, the old saying goes "Don't repeat yourself" — do repetitive things only once. Constantly writing your own info and bio is, in a sense, repeating yourself (lol).

As a frontend engineer, building your own website should be routine. So organizing your portfolio nicely and presenting it as a list isn't hard, right? But at this point the "super passionate" frontend devs vanish. Everyone swears they're passionate about frontend, yet their GitHub is empty (though I won't rule out private projects or stuff on other platforms). So be an honest person like me — if you don't have one, just don't claim you do. If you're passionate, it'll show naturally; no need to fake it.

After building my resume and portfolio site, plus a number of **original** and **collaborative** projects — honestly, I could talk about each one for an hour. I also implore everyone: **please don't copy portfolios**. If you do that, your resume response rate will go way up. I've handed you the secret — don't say I'm hoarding it XD.

- Algorithms

Many frontend devs hate grinding problems. Me too!

First off: I hate "grinding," but I like the feeling of "solving." I mostly use JavaScript and TypeScript — also the main frontend languages — so you can get familiar with the language and the logic at the same time. Differences between languages are getting smaller now (of course there's still dynamic vs. static, strong vs. weak typing). What others can do, JavaScript can do too (wait — I'm not trying to start a language war). Using it for problems is totally fine; you don't have to copy everyone else and use Python, C++, etc. (if you really must pick, pick C++).

I think grinding problems isn't about practicing specific languages — it's about practicing logical thinking. The low-level details are just details, like whether to declare a variable with `let`, `const`, `int`, `bool`, `char*` — just use what you know. Later you can challenge yourself with a 2nd or 3rd language.

On 2023/01/15 I went to the [2023 Yourator Digital Career Expo](https://www.accupass.com/event/2211140558381956060137). I asked everyone whether frontend interviews test LeetCode. The answer was mostly **no, or at most one easy**. If you really hate grinding, don't grind — but then you need the corresponding implementation experience to prove your ability.

- Vim

Many frontend devs hate using Vim. Me too!

By "hate" I mean when I first learned Vim — even quitting (`:q`) took forever. Moving one character at a time (`hjkl`), entering insert mode only with `i`, saving and quitting only with (`:wq`). But with just that you can complete **all tasks** — it just... takes longer.

After seeing all kinds of god-tier maneuvers from the greats and practicing hard, I genuinely started Vim from the state above — slowly writing code in it. Eventually I could fly around in Vim, use various scripts, with operations flowing like water. Writing frontend with the ancient artifact is just drip-drip cool (X.

Learning Vim seems like it wouldn't help much. Example: in Vim `:q` means quit, and lots of software follows that convention — e.g., typing `q` under `git log` also quits. And when you remote into a server, the default editor is Vim — at that moment, you'll thank your past self for having learned Vim!

On Twitter or in groups, whenever editor wars break out, you'll see the line **real pro use vim** — meaning professional devs develop in Vim. Currently, hardly anyone in frontend develops in Vim (wait — not trying to start an editor war), and there are more and more good tools that can do what Vim does. Whether to learn it is up to you.

In short, anything that **boosts your productivity** is a good thing. You could also try Emacs or IntelliJ — both solid choices — but I haven't really explored those two.

- Quick summary

Notice: **when others hate something, do that thing** — that'll be your edge. Of course you also have to put in the effort to learn it. What's excellence? It's **the ability to make others mediocre**. I agree with that. You might think I'm being "invy" (involuting), but there's no help for it — sorry, that's how the world works. The rarer and more in-demand your skills, the more senior talent you are. Or think about it: what's the difference between Junior and Senior? With similar years of experience, why is the Senior someone else and not you?

Wait! You say you also hate CSS layout? That's fine — then try writing backend SQL?

There's an engineer joke that goes: "Frontend can't lay out CSS; backend can't write SQL."

Of course, lots of tools "package" these things up. A not-entirely-fair example: Bootstrap replaces layout; ORM replaces SQL. But when you can't modify Bootstrap, or can't write a simple CRUD SQL query, then you'll understand. Did you really think engineers just copy/paste and sip coffee all day for a paycheck? Where do such bubbles come from? Let me pop them for you. Or — if a job like that really exists, please tell me immediately whether they're hiring (huh?).

## What I Did

- Kept chasing **new tech**, and filled in the **old tech** you're missing.

In software, the only constant is change. This has been said to death, especially in frontend — frameworks and tools change insanely fast! Fall behind and you get phased out. React is already at 18 — is jQuery alone enough? Vite builds lightning fast, Vue 3 is also tempting — not gonna try?

Chasing new tech is par for the course for devs. But what about old tech? **Computer fundamentals, data structures, algorithms.** Funny thing — these are age-old subjects, yet few want to learn them. Compared to new tech, these should be the highest-ROI skills, yet everyone is busy chasing the new stuff, or even arguing about which new tech is best. If you've got time to argue, spend it on self-improvement. If you've got these skills, why argue at all?

CS majors trained for 4 years for their kung fu — did you really think you'd catch up in 3–6 months? If you still have time, properly fill in these foundational tech skills. The career-change halo will run out one day. Future you, job-hopping, will thank past you (so tongue-twisty). Keep your eyes on the long game.

- Read classic developer books (Clean Code, etc.)

After the switch, I didn't slack off. I usually read before work — I don't really eat breakfast anyway. With a 09:00 start, I'd typically get to the office around 08:50 and read a bit, around 10–15 minutes a day. I always keep 2–3 books at my desk. Cumulatively, I've read:

Sometimes I also read non-programming stuff. Let me tell you another little secret: people nowadays barely read, so if you read, you're already half-winning (huh?). **Good reading habits give you an edge in any field.**

- Follow the greats

Before transitioning, I'd occasionally read programming articles. The most pivotal one for me was the great Huli's: [10 Years of Self-Taught Programming Reflection](https://buzzorange.com/techorange/2016/04/13/self-study-program-with-datastructure-and-algorithm/). I saw it around late 2018. I was still a security guard then, thinking "wow, self-study takes ten years!" — really not easy. Wait — who's this guy? Weird name. Huli? Is it a screen name? Related to Hu Gua?

> Further reading: [Huli's 10 Years of Self-Taught Programming | TechOrange](https://buzzorange.com/techorange/2016/04/13/self-study-program-with-datastructure-and-algorithm/)

My job required day/night shift rotation. That day I happened to be on the night shift. There was plenty of time before off-duty and the sky wasn't bright yet (any job related to aviation almost always requires shift work — think about it: planes fly 24 hours, right? Then someone has to be there 24 hours — exactly). Let me check out this guy's blog. Don't look — actually look, and wow. This guy is about my age, didn't finish college, and was already a senior engineer!

I'm genuinely grateful to the great Huli for sharing his story — just like I'm sharing mine now — and making me feel this path was viable. So right then I **made a choice** and **executed on it hard**. I studied applied math at a private university, and I think my math is OK (perfect SAT-equivalent math score — don't ask why I still went to a private school). Career change seemed doable, so I self-studied while working. I'd long since lost the ability to tell day from night anyway. Later I joined full-time study to strengthen things more, and I've been strengthening ever since.

After I started working, mostly with Vue, I read the docs and discovered Vue core member — the great **antfu**. Top 100 on GitHub. I won't say more — really strong and low-key. He's open-sourced tons of tools (some you may have used) and shared tons of articles and resources, all open source.

At the 2022 Vue X Laravel Conf I attended the Vue track and made a point to get antfu's autograph. He even said he'd never signed one before, but signed it for me anyway (blush). Yes! That's how I got the **first-ever antfu autograph**! It's kind of that feeling of treating greats like pop stars!

> Learn more: [Laravel x Vue Conf Taiwan 2022](https://laravelconf.tw/)

Anyway, pick a few greats in your field (or people around you work too) to follow — it gives you more motivation. It's like **a lamp lighting the way for you**. When you're lost or want to give up, it's still lit.

- Quick summary

So what makes me different from other frontend devs? Not much, really — at work I still lay out CSS, hook up APIs, write docs. Am I senior? What's the **definition of senior** anyway? I don't know — and it doesn't seem that important. I think **being able to keep improving is the most important thing.** Why get hung up on a title?

## Am I Happy

- Is being a dev a great job?

Honestly, I don't feel anything special. **Dev is a job; security guard is a job.** As jobs go, I don't think there's high or low. **Are there security guards who take it seriously?** Definitely. Like the guard downstairs at R — really nice guy, says hi to everyone. I'd buy him a drink from time to time. **Are there devs who slack off?** No need to tell me — plenty of people feel this deeply.

The point is how you do the job.

When I worked at an aviation security outfit (aka security guard), I saw plenty of faces. Cocky captains who'd take or leave their parking permit like the company was their private lot. Polite flight instructors (the ones who train captains — the kind who can make a captain "fly up," physically fly up) who often checked on us — "it's cold, wear more" — and sometimes bought us drinks. Flight attendant candidates who all looked the part, but some would take or leave their documents when we verified them (actually, most of us security folks were tight with the assistants and VPs; one VP even told us: anyone who doesn't listen, just write down their number for us, tell them not to come back to interview — we were basically the hidden first round of the interview). OK, I drifted off there — back to topic. I'm grateful for that job too; maybe it's precisely because I had it that I worked so hard. Or maybe I was always used to grinding. I don't know.

One more thing — I usually don't call myself an engineer, because the word's been overused. You've probably met that "I'm also an engineer, you know" friend, only to chat a bit and find out they can't write a single programming language. It's not about looking down on them (if anything, web frontend is probably the bottom of the pecking chain QQ). It's just that continuing the conversation gets really awkward — you have to figure out how to keep them flexing without bursting their bubble. Anyone been there? Making conversation is an art.

- So how's the current job?

The current job is genuinely hard. I might be a glutton for punishment — knowing there are tigers on the mountain, I go anyway, just to see how hard it can get. Treat hardship like a supplement. While I'm still young, more practice opportunities. Maybe the company takes off later (definitely my illusion — illusion).

Since it's fully remote, on my first day part-timing, the full-stack guy in charge of tech said: "Ping xxx.xx.xx.xx first, check the latency."

A typical junior frontend might think: Huh? Ping? Ping what? Don't play fisticuffs with him, try cutting mid?

Luckily I'd read a bit of [鳥哥的 Linux 私房菜](https://linux.vbird.org/). I immediately opened the terminal and ran the command, replying: "Latency's about 160 ms right now."

> Further reading: [鳥哥私房菜 - Chapter 5, Common Linux Network Commands, ping](https://linux.vbird.org/linux_server/centos6/0140networkcommand.php#ping)

The full-stack guy replied coolly: "Not bad. Let's develop like this then."

And then, eating hotpot and singing songs (a reference to the movie _Let the Bullets Fly_), he directly assigned tasks and we started work. From December (2022/12) all the way through Chinese New Year (2023/02), I was working overtime — coding 12+ hours a day (full-time + part-time combined).

- So where's that seven-figure salary, you ask?

Here it comes.

C's offer is about 50,000 NZD per year (NZD to TWD is about 1:19). The company is registered in New Zealand, so it pays in NZD. Some might think: wow, foreign company! Don't assume "not in Taiwan" means foreign — the founder and core members are mainland Chinese, with colleagues in India too. It's just that kind of company.

Converting NZD, that's about 950k TWD / year — not bad. Given the workload, really not bad.

Hold on a sec! That's not seven figures! And you call yourself honest? Liar!

Let the bullets fly for a while. Look carefully at the timeline above. I actually started "part-timing" at C last year (2022/12) for 2 months. "Part-time" here means **20 hours a week** (half a full-time role) of work, so it's effectively **one extra month** of salary.

So 950k plus about 70k part-time pay = 1.02 million. Whether I can actually last a full year is another question (huh?). No need to say it again — **I'm an honest person** (gets beaten) — if it's seven figures, it's seven figures.

That's about it. Hope this reflection — about I-don't-know-what-the-hell-I-was-writing — helped. See you next time ~
