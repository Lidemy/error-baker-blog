Hey everyone, 2023 is wrapping up — how are you all?

For me, it's the same old busy-busy. End of year, you know how it is. Feels like I've been dark for a bit — checking back in to let you know I'm still alive ~ 😂

This post is split into 3 sections, as in the title: Goals, Efficiency, Balance.

- **Goals** => for readers in a career-change phase or a job-hopping phase. If you're just about to switch or still hesitating, it might be a bit tough, but I'll share my pre-transition goals and how goals affected me — hope it helps.

- **Efficiency** => This section introduces tools, plugins, and apps I use, and how I boost my efficiency. (Here I'll plant a flag: usually with clickbaity titles like this, the author isn't an engineer, and their understanding of what engineers mean by **efficiency** is pretty off. They easily produce content that sounds reasonable — like "distinguish important/not important, urgent/not urgent" blah blah — but the actual help is limited. My experience is often: everything's important, everything's urgent. What do you do then?) Of course, the efficiency angle here will be learning / programming-related. As always, take what helps you.

- **Balance** => For me 2023 has been a relatively imbalanced year — lots of overtime, including meetings that often run for hours. The latest day went until 11 PM. It's a very small startup with no proper PM (more like a part-time one), so most responsibilities fall on the devs.

My first day after the career change was `2020/11/15`. Counting to `2023/12/31`, that's a bit over 2 years.

> Or, more precisely: 2.126027397260274 years

Inevitably, the frontend market now is getting harder. Not just because of the pandemic and ChatGPT — but also the "many monks, little porridge" situation is more obvious. Senior frontenders used to be genuinely rare, but many juniors from back then have slowly climbed up, which means fewer and fewer companies are willing to take on juniors with no experience. If you're mid-change or thinking about changing, you may need to speed up. From my view, the current situation is roughly:

- 4 years ago (2018–2020), knowing some HTML, CSS, JavaScript was enough to switch successfully.
- 2 years ago (2021–2022), if you'd touched a framework and your HTML/CSS/JS weren't too bad, you could still get an offer.
- Now (2023–2024), HTML/CSS/JS aside, you need to be fluent in a framework, TypeScript, maybe some backend experience — and even then you might not get an offer.

This is in the safe scenario. Because frontend tech changes fast, if you don't meet the bar, you can send hundreds of resumes and only get a few replies. Reaching the level above improves your reply rate and offer odds, so you don't take too much of a confidence hit and stall out.

I've also been chatting with senior frontend friends lately, and even they feel starting from zero in frontend is hard at this moment. For those who are less confident but want to enter the IT industry, you could start as QA (tester) — at least get into the industry to feel it out.

## On Goals

- Goals before crystallizing

<center>
  <img src="https://hackmd.io/_uploads/B1_GJKhwa.png" class="post-image-width" style="width: 480px" alt="2020 年度目標" />
</center>

> The goals I wrote down in 2020, before the career change. A bit chuunibyou, but still effective.

As the saying goes, "nothing is hard in this world if you set your mind to it." I think with determination, the chance of a successful career change is actually pretty high. The key is your goal — what do you want to achieve? Is the goal feasible? What effort are you willing to put in?

At that time I hadn't even started the career change, and I read the teacher's article about a great-sounding engineering job — this was the result I wanted.

> Further reading: [Huli - Half a Year Working at Insurtech OneDegree | Medium](https://hulitw.medium.com/working-at-onedegree-378a0401130e) (Note: As of 2023/11, Huli is working in Japan ~)

I didn't doubt whether I could do it — I definitely could. It was just a **matter of time**. So right then, I also set a medium-to-long-term goal.

Medium-to-long-term goal: in 3–5 years, reach the state the teacher was in at OneDegree (4 years / 100k). `Monthly salary 100k`. Goal clear!

I won't dwell too much on the career-change story here — my cohort classmates and close partners understand better. Some people still think being an engineer looks easy for me, that the pay is decent, that I can work remotely, hang out in cafés, etc. But the ups and downs — only people who've been through it really get. During the transition, time-wise it was full-time study, but I approached it like more-than-full-time. Average people study about 40 hours a week; I was over 50 hours a week, learning extra algorithms, frameworks, and so on.

As for "imposter syndrome" — it hasn't happened to me. I think it's just: don't overthink it, charge ahead. Lots of people overthink but don't act; some have high ambitions but low effort. Just stay curious, keep a learning mindset, and you'll be less likely to get imposter syndrome.

- No pain, No gain.

To get high pay, to make money — I don't think anything isn't hard work. The principle behind high pay is simple: **high irreplaceability**. Look at easy office jobs — most fresh grads can do them, so naturally the pay isn't high.

This reminds me of a high-school teacher I had — very knowledgeable and funny. In high school when you're applying to university, lots of students ask the teacher what major to pick, where to go.

Quite a few students directly ask: "Teacher teacher, which major makes the most money?"

The teacher replied: "I don't know either."

The students froze.

Then the teacher continued: "If you know, or if you find out later, please come back and tell me. I'd like to know too."

"What used to make money doesn't mean it makes money now; what makes money now doesn't mean it'll make money in the future."

"If you're interested in teaching or being a teacher, come chat with me."

"But whatever the major, university study will only be harder. Make sure you're really ready before you go, and don't waste your parents' money!"

"In this life, no stranger will support you unconditionally — except your parents. And parents, frankly, are just strangers with a blood tie."

Overall, that teacher influenced me a lot, in many ways — reading, for instance. He'd often share books with us. He'd often mention *Don't Eat the Marshmallow... Yet!*, which influenced me a lot, and *The Secret*, which I only read after hearing him introduce it.

- Looking back, did I hit my goal this year?

Now, a bit over 2 years into the career change, did I hit 100k?

Honestly, only "close." Earlier (around early December), I interviewed at a large gambling company, and the offer came in a bit above my goal. I'd genuinely been planning to go (why say no to money QQ), but when I resigned, my boss talked me into staying. We talked a long time — should've been about 3 hours. The front part was more about company prospects; mainly, the company's product I still like better than gambling. Anyway, starting in March (2024), there'll be a raise, and they'll let me try being the Frontend team leader. Since it's still a small startup that hasn't really started making money, I'll treat bonuses/dividends as zero. The new raise contract has an NDA, but if I do collect everything smoothly, by all calculations, I should hit the goal.

Coming back to "irreplaceability" — the team currently has only 2 frontend devs, and I'm one of them. I work on the more core parts, so of course I'm more tired (or maybe that's just me). But the fact that the boss was willing to raise pay to keep me suggests the boss does see how much you put in. I breathed a sigh of relief. It's not a very high-paying job right now, but the future potential is higher, so I figured I'd grind a bit more and see.

Don't be afraid of being tired or doing extra, because **what you put in may not show up immediately, but it all becomes chips for your future**. If the boss or manager has eyes, they'll see. Since joining, I've carried that belief: deliver and deliver fast, proactively take on the hard parts. The result has consistently been that it comes back as chips in some form. Tried and true — that's about the story.

## On Efficiency

- What did I do for efficiency?

First I confess: **I bought quite a few keyboards**.

Though it's less related to tech, I think it's genuinely worth it. For now, my top recommendation is still the engineer's weapon: HHKB (Happy Hacking Keyboard — if money's OK, just buy this without thinking).

But because I wanted more customization, I bought a mechanical keyboard with a similar layout, and alternate occasionally.

Whenever you hear the word "cus-tom-ize," you know it's a deep rabbit hole. As an engineer, I'm sure you also dread those three syllables. I won't go deep into that area here (sweat).

- Some "niche-but-efficient" tech

For efficiency I also learned `Vim` and the `Boshiamy input method`. These two make my note-taking and doc-writing super fast. When planning flows, I draft on HackMD first — because besides being cross-platform, HackMD has a Vim mode! Vim mode! Vim mode! Important enough to say three times.

> Further reading: interested readers can check out my earlier [Boshiamy post](https://blog.errorbaker.tw/posts/benben/13-boshiamy/)

For editors, it's mostly `VS Code`. I once jumped to `NeoVim` to play with it, but the config was too fiddly, and lots of plugins are only on `VS Code`, so I went back to `VS Code`. I also installed the Vim plugin in `VS Code`. Earlier there was no `NeoVim` support, but later it came! In `VS Code` you can develop as fast as Vim/NeoVim but with the plugin ecosystem. At this point, all my pain points are satisfied!

But kids make choices — I want it all! For now, I use editors like this:

1. Larger projects => `VS Code`
2. Smaller projects (like grinding problems, notes) => `NeoVim`

Here are some plugins and apps I find good for efficiency. I'll skip the common ones — you're probably sick of seeing them.

> VS Code plugins

- [Console Ninja](https://console-ninja.com/): A `console.log` master's best friend. Using `console.log` isn't shameful — what's shameful is flipping back and forth just to find a `console.log('hi')`.
- [CodeSnap](https://marketplace.visualstudio.com/items?itemName=adpyke.codesnap): A handy tool for snapping code images, so engineers who can't explain in words become more persuasive, and even bad code looks less bad.
- [Image Preview](https://marketplace.visualstudio.com/items?itemName=kisstkondoros.vscode-gutter-preview): Quickly view image thumbnails in VS Code.
- [Vim](https://marketplace.visualstudio.com/items?itemName=vscodevim.vim): As a Vim user, I have to recommend the Vim plugin in VS Code (X)
- [NERDTree](https://marketplace.visualstudio.com/items?itemName=Llam4u.nerdtree): A Vim-related feature; you can operate files in VS Code like [Vim nerd tree](https://github.com/preservim/nerdtree)

> Browser plugins

- [GlobalSpeed](https://github.com/polywock/globalSpeed): Set playback speed in the browser. E.g., Netflix caps at 1.5x — you can customize faster or slower. Super handy for tutorials, since some platforms don't even have a speed option.
- [Dark Reader](https://darkreader.org/): Force dark mode. Great for sites without dark mode, with lots of customization.
- [Picture-in-Picture any site](https://chromewebstore.google.com/detail/picture-in-picture-any-si/fgopnhbjlphjjcfbapfcbakjekpffkff): Let **any site** use Picture-in-Picture. Usually only videos have it, but this makes note-taking situations more convenient.
- [Vimium](https://chromewebstore.google.com/detail/vimium/dbepggeogbaibhgnhhndojpepiihcmeb): Lets you browse sites Vim-style in the browser.

> Apps (mostly Mac)

- [Free | Warp](https://app.warp.dev/referral/VLL959): Super handy terminal, out of the box. See the [post I wrote earlier](https://blog.errorbaker.tw/posts/benben/12-warp/)
- [Free | Raycast](https://www.raycast.com/): Mac's super-powerful productivity tool with tons of features and local dev support. Planning to write a post on it (probably)
- [Free | Homerow](https://github.com/dexterleng/homerow): Another Vim-style operation tool. The free version pops a paid prompt about every 50 uses but doesn't affect usage (casually gathering a group buy — DM me if interested 😂)
- [Paid | Xnapper](https://xnapper.com/): Screenshot artifact — say goodbye to crooked, ugly screenshots.
- [Paid | DevUtils](https://devutils.com/): A combined dev toolkit — Regex, various beautify, various conversions, etc.

> PS. Both paid tools I bought during Black Friday at half price. Interested readers might want to wait for another sale!

## On Balance

Feels like everything is better with balance. Maybe right now you lean to one side, but that's fine — each phase is different.

- Work vs Life

For a long time, my state has been: `work (coding) >>> life`.

Since February, I've worked Saturdays almost every week, sometimes Sundays too. But I've gotten kind of used to it — when a day off comes, I don't even know what to do. When that thought came up, I realized I should rebalance (sweat).

Yeah, it's probably time to balance. When negotiating the new offer with the boss, I mentioned the time-off part. After the new year, Saturday overtime will be every other week, not continuous, and they'll hire more people to share the load.

Tiring as it is, looking back, lots of people are still grinding and practicing problems. Meanwhile, lots of people are "lying flat" (accidentally fired a map-wide shot). Maybe everyone just pursues different things — no absolute right or wrong.

Later, for life balance, two months ago I picked up Bass again — one of this year's happy events. I figured I'd find something less programming-related for balance. I often ask myself: take away work, take away profession — what's left of me? Probably that's why I've cultivated a lot of interests.

I'll keep half-lying like this — it's also nice. Hopefully I can lie flat more in the future.

- "Wage-earner" mindset vs "building a business" mindset

This difference in mindset is something I've felt more since joining this job. As mentioned, this job has lots of overtime. But when I work overtime, the boss and partners are all there too — because it's their business. They have the "building a business" mindset.

Some people finish their work and leave right on the dot. Switching jobs may get you raises, but each switch may restart from zero — you often end up with "5 years experience" that's actually "1 year experience repeated 5 times." That's the "wage-earner" mindset.

But the "building a business" mindset is different. One year might give you 5x the experience, because you hit so many problems and have to figure out how to overcome them even with scarce resources. It's high-challenge, high-growth. Will you be tired? Of course! But push through and it's yours.

I also often hear that the boss and partners have health issues, go to the hospital, come right back to meetings and work. I can only say: starting a business is really not easy!

But working till you check into the hospital is a bit much. Lots of internet companies in mainland China really do "996, 007" — pretty scary. Suddenly I feel that just being an overtime wage-drone isn't so bad (no it's not).

- Finally

Really, as above, in everything seek affinity — I mean, seek "balance."

Lots of things need balance to last long. The art of balance!

Work, career change, life, practicing an instrument — same goes.

Anyway, grateful for another year in software development. Maybe in the early years of a career there's more ideas and passion to write, so I'll share more while I still have things to share!
